# Static Stretching

## Definition / Explanation
Static stretching involves moving a joint through its full range of motion and then performing a stretch by holding that position for a period of time (typically 15 to 60 seconds). It is primarily used to increase muscle-tendon unit length and improve long-term flexibility. ^[raw/Low_Impact_and_Functional_Fitness_Over_50.md]

## Current State of Knowledge
While widely practiced, current research suggests that static stretching may have different impacts depending on when it is performed. Performing lengthy static stretches immediately before high-intensity strength training can potentially lead to a temporary decrease in power output. However, when used as part of a cool-down or as a standalone flexibility session, it is an effective tool for improving range of motion and promoting relaxation. ^[raw/cardiac-recovery-fitness-protocol-deep-research.md]

For cardiac rehabilitation patients, static stretching is generally reserved for the cool-down phase rather than the warm-up, where dynamic flexibility work is preferred for nervous-system activation. ^[raw/cardiac-recovery-fitness-protocol-deep-research.md]

## Safety Notes for Cardiac Recovery
- Avoid holding stretches beyond 60 seconds in early Phase II recovery — prolonged passive stretch combined with Valsalva can spike blood pressure.
- Never hold breath during a static stretch; exhale steadily through the held position.
- Stop immediately if dizziness, chest pressure, or unusual shortness of breath occurs.

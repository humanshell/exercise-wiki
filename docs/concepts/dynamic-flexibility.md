# Dynamic Flexibility

## Definition / Explanation
Dynamic flexibility refers to the ability to move joints through a full range of motion with control and coordination. Unlike static stretching (holding a position), dynamic movement involves active muscle contractions that transition a joint through its functional path. This type of mobility is highly relevant for warming up and preparing the body for physical activity. ^[raw/cardiac-recovery-fitness-protocol-deep-research.md]

## Current State of Knowledge
Current research suggests that dynamic mobilization helps increase intra-muscular temperature and nervous system readiness without diminishing muscle power — a potential drawback of prolonged static stretching immediately before high-intensity training. For individuals focusing on long-term health and injury prevention, mastering these movement patterns is foundational for maintaining functional mobility. ^[raw/cardiac-recovery-fitness-protocol-deep-research.md]

Dynamic flexibility exercises are preferred during the warm-up phase of a cardiac rehabilitation session because they activate the sympathetic nervous system, raise heart rate gradually, and prepare the cardiovascular system for more demanding activity without imposing excessive myocardial oxygen demand. ^[raw/cardiac-recovery-fitness-protocol-deep-research.md]

## Role in Cardiac Rehabilitation
In the Phase II–III outpatient setting, dynamic warm-up typically precedes aerobic exercise. Recommended movements include:
- Large-joint circles (ankles, hips, shoulders)
- Walking gait progressions (leg swings, arm circles)
- Controlled articular rotations (CAT) for spine
- Light resistance band pull-aparts for upper back activation

Duration: 5–10 minutes, building from low to moderate intensity.

## Safety Notes for Cardiac Recovery
- Keep tempo controlled — ballistic or jerky movements can spike systolic blood pressure.
- Limit range of motion to comfortable, pain-free arcs; joint proprioception may be reduced in early recovery due to medication effects (e.g., beta-blockers).
- Cease immediately if exertional symptoms appear: chest pressure, lightheadedness, unusual dyspnea.

# **Physiology and Risks of the Valsalva Maneuver in Cardiac Recovery**

* **Source URL:** [https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537248/](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK537248/) & [https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/322661](https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/322661)  
* **Document Type:** Clinical Pathophysiology and Safety Analysis

## **Executive Summary**

The Valsalva maneuver is a particular breathing technique involving forceful exhalation against a closed glottis. It triggers profound hemodynamic changes and shifts in blood pressure. While it has diagnostic and clinical applications (such as terminating supraventricular tachycardia), performing it involuntarily during resistance training presents serious risks for cardiac patients.

## **The Four Hemodynamic Phases of the Valsalva Maneuver**

1. **Phase I (Onset of Strain):** As the patient bears down, intrathoracic pressure rises, squeezing blood out of the pulmonary circulation and into the aorta. This causes a brief, sharp increase in systemic blood pressure.  
2. **Phase II (Sustained Strain):** Prolonged thoracic pressure restricts venous return (preload) to the heart, causing a drop in cardiac output and a fall in blood pressure. Baroreceptors in the carotid artery detect this drop, triggering a reflex narrowing of blood vessels and a significant increase in heart rate to compensate.  
3. **Phase III (Release of Strain):** When the breath is suddenly released, pressure in the chest drops rapidly, causing a brief, further dip in blood pressure and a transient heart rate increase.  
4. **Phase IV (Recovery Period):** Venous blood rushes back into the heart. Cardiac output surges, causing systemic blood pressure to overshoot its baseline level. This overshoot triggers a reflex slowing of the heart rate (vagal response) before blood pressure returns to normal.

## **Risks and Contraindications in Cardiac Rehabilitation**

Because the Valsalva maneuver dramatically reduces cardiac output and creates rapid blood pressure fluctuations, it is highly risky for patients with underlying heart conditions.

* **Clinical Risks:** It can cause acute hypotension, lightheadedness, syncope, chest pain, arrhythmias, and, in rare cases, stroke.  
* **Absolute Contraindications:** The maneuver must be strictly avoided by patients with a history of coronary artery disease, valvular disease, congenital heart disease, unstable heart failure, retinopathy, or recent intraocular lens implantation.

## **Valsalva Prevention Mechanics**

To prevent these dangerous cardiovascular spikes during exercise, patients must be taught correct breathing biomechanics :

* **Continuous Exhalation:** Patients must never hold their breath during exertion.  
* **Breathing Pattern:** Instruct patients to inhale through the nose during the eccentric phase (lowering the weight) and actively exhale through an open mouth during the concentric phase (lifting the weight), exhaling fluidly through the sticking point of the movement.

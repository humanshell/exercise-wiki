# **Entry Timelines, Sternal Precautions, and Contraindications for Resistance Training**

* **Source URL:** [https://academic.oup.com/eurjcn/article/25/1/198/8443015](https://academic.oup.com/eurjcn/article/25/1/198/8443015) & [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1484533/](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1484533/)  
* **Document Type:** Clinical Systematic Review and Timeline Guidelines

## **Executive Summary**

While aerobic training is highly prioritized early in recovery, incorporating resistance training (RT) is critical to prevent muscle deconditioning, treat sarcopenia, and improve quality of life. This document establishes the clinical entry criteria for RT across different cardiac diagnoses, details essential sternal precautions following open-heart surgery, and reviews device-specific loading limits.

## **Clinical Timelines for Resuming/Starting Resistance Training**

* **Myocardial Infarction (MI):**  
  * **AACVPR Guideline:** Permitted to start with 1 to 3 lb hand weights on rehabilitation entry. Traditional resistance training can begin at 5 weeks post-MI, provided the patient has completed at least 4 weeks of structured, supervised aerobic training.  
  * **ACSM Guideline:** Traditional resistance training is delayed until 3 months post-event, though light 1 to 3 lb hand weights may be used starting 2 weeks post-MI.  
* **Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI):**  
  * **AACVPR Guideline:** 1 to 3 lb hand weights upon program entry. Traditional resistance training is permitted at 3 weeks post-PCI if the patient has completed at least 2 weeks of supervised aerobic training.  
  * **ACSM Guideline:** Traditional resistance training is safe to initiate after completing 2 weeks of structured aerobic exercise.  
* **Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG) / Median Sternotomy:**  
  * **Standard Guideline:** Resistance training of the lower body can begin early, but upper body resistance training must be delayed for 6 to 8 weeks (and up to 12 weeks historically) to protect the healing sternum from unexpected mechanical torque and reduce the risk of sternal instability, wound dehiscence, or bone infection.

## **Post-Operative Sternal and Device Precautions**

### **Sternal Precautions (Weeks 1 to 6 Post-Op)**

Following a median sternotomy, the closed sternum is strong enough for light, natural arm movements, but strict limits must be maintained to prevent incisional strain :

1. **Lifting Limit:** Strict ban on lifting items heavier than 5 to 10 lbs.  
2. **Arm Mechanics:** Avoid pushing, pulling, reaching behind the back, or bilateral overhead arm movements.  
3. **Range of Motion:** Keep upper body range-of-motion exercises close to the body to minimize torque across the chest wall.

### **Pacemaker and Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator (ICD) Precautions**

To protect the newly implanted device and lead wires:

1. **Arm Movement Limit:** The patient must not raise the affected arm above shoulder height for 2 weeks post-procedure.  
2. **Lifting Limit:** Ban on lifting items heavier than 10 lbs on the affected side for 4 weeks.
